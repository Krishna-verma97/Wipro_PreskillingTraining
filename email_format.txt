Case Study 1: Direct vs. Indirect Communication
A German project manager (known for direct and straightforward communication) is leading a multicultural team that includes Japanese members. In a meeting, he openly criticizes mistakes in the project. The Japanese members feel uncomfortable, interpreting this as disrespectful and loss of face, while the manager sees it as “just being honest.”

Discussion Points:
How do direct vs. indirect communication styles cause conflict?
What strategies can the manager adopt to maintain honesty while being culturally sensitive?


Case Study 2: Decision-Making Styles
An American team prefers quick decision-making and moving fast, while their Indian counterparts want to take more time to consult internally before finalizing. The Americans feel the Indians are “too slow,” and the Indians feel the Americans are “too hasty.”

Discussion Points:
How do cultural attitudes towards speed and thoroughness impact collaboration?
What middle ground can be created so both teams feel respected?


Case Study 3: Leadership Expectations
A Scandinavian manager promotes a flat hierarchy and encourages everyone to call her by first name and challenge her ideas. The team in China, however, feels uncomfortable, as they are used to hierarchical respect and prefer clear top-down instructions.

Discussion Points:
How do different cultures view leadership and hierarchy?
How can the leader adjust her style to build trust with all team members?



Case Study 4: Time Orientation Conflict
A Brazilian supplier often delivers reports later than the agreed timeline, assuming relationships and flexibility matter more than strict punctuality. Their Swiss client, however, is frustrated as punctuality is considered professionalism.

Discussion Points:
How do different views on time (flexible vs. strict) create conflicts?
How can deadlines be managed to respect cultural differences while ensuring business efficiency?


Case Study 5: Team Collaboration Across Cultures
A global software team includes members from the US, Japan, and the Middle East. In brainstorming meetings, Americans share ideas openly, while Japanese members remain quiet, waiting for a turn, and Middle Eastern members prefer to build personal rapport before diving into tasks. The Americans assume others are “not contributing,” while others feel Americans are “too dominating.”

Discussion Points:
How do cultural approaches to teamwork and idea-sharing vary?
What facilitation methods can ensure balanced contributions?


Case Study 6: Gender and Cultural Expectations
A female manager from Canada is leading a project in the Middle East. Some local team members are hesitant to accept her authority because of traditional cultural norms. She notices resistance and less cooperation compared to when male colleagues lead.

Discussion Points:
How do gender and cultural norms intersect in global teams?
What respectful approaches can the manager use to assert leadership while navigating cultural sensitivity?

Case Study 1: Declining Sales in a Key Product
Scenario:
Your company’s flagship product, which contributes to 40% of the revenue, has seen a 20% decline in sales over the last two quarters. Competitors have launched similar products at a lower price.
Task for the Team:
Analyze possible causes (pricing, customer preferences, marketing gaps, product relevance).
Propose a strategy to regain market share.
Decide whether to invest in innovation, marketing, or price cuts.

Case Study 2: Cross-Department Conflict
Scenario:
The sales team blames operations for late deliveries, while the operations team blames sales for unrealistic timelines. This conflict is lowering team morale and customer satisfaction.
Task for the Team:
Identify the root cause of the conflict.
Create a plan to improve inter-departmental coordination.
Suggest a long-term process to avoid recurrence.


Case Study 3: Ethical Dilemma in Vendor Selection
Scenario:
A vendor who has supplied materials to your company for 10 years is suddenly offering slightly higher prices than a new vendor. However, the new vendor has no proven track record and is offering an attractive deal.
Task for the Team:
Decide whether to stay loyal to the old vendor or take the risk with the new one.
Discuss ethical, financial, and long-term reliability factors.
Present a collective decision with justifications.

Case Study 4: Employee Retention Crisis
Scenario:
Your company’s top-performing employees are leaving for competitors offering better work-life balance and career growth opportunities. The attrition rate has increased by 15%.
Task for the Team:
Identify the main reasons employees may be leaving.
Suggest immediate retention strategies.
Propose long-term cultural or policy changes to build loyalty.

Case Study 5: Technology Upgrade Debate
Scenario:
The IT department suggests implementing a new AI-based system to improve efficiency. However, the system is expensive and requires training. Some leaders believe the current system is “good enough.”
Task for the Team:
Debate the pros and cons of adopting new technology.
Decide whether to invest now, delay, or reject.
Present a financial and operational justification for the decision.

Case Study 6: Crisis Communication Challenge
Scenario:
A negative article about your company’s environmental impact has gone viral on social media. Customers are threatening boycotts, and stakeholders are demanding a response.
Task for the Team:
Formulate an immediate crisis communication plan.
Decide how to address media, customers, and employees.
Propose a long-term CSR/environmental strategy to rebuild reputation.
